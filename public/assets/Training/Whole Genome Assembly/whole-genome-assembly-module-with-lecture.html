    INTRODUCTION

    Advancements in technology allowed sequencing of the entire genome, generating large volumes of genomic data at decreasing cost. The development of next-generation sequencing (NGS) and third-generation long-read platforms has significantly increased sequencing speed, accuracy, and read length compared to traditional Sanger sequencing. These improvements have expanded applications in diverse fields, such as evolutionary biology, conservation genetics, agriculture, aquaculture, medicine, and biotechnology. As a result, researchers can now investigate genome structure, genetic variation, and complex traits at unprecedented resolution.

    For most of the next-generation and third-generation sequencing platforms, the generated sequences are fragments of the genome and usually require an assembly process. Whole genome assembly is a process of reconstructing the genome sequence of an organism from short or long DNA sequences called reads, produced from DNA sequencing platforms. Whole genome assembly workflows may vary, but can be summarized into its general steps: Raw Data QC, Trimming, Sequence Assembly, and Assembly QC. The goal is to generate a contiguous and accurate representation of the genome which will be used for further analysis.

    To provide biological meaning to the genome assembled, genome annotation is usually performed. Genome annotation is the process of identifying, labelling and mapping functional elements, such as genes and non-coding sequences, in a DNA sequence. This allows researchers to study the genome, including its gene composition and corresponding potential biological functions.

    This module will teach the basic concepts of whole genome assembly and genome annotation using command-line interface (CLI) tools and sample datasets. Specifically, this module was designed with the following objectives:

    1. Learn how to navigate basic command line in Linux
    2. Understand the fundamental principles of whole-genome sequencing (WGS) and annotation
    3. Execute an end-to-end whole genome assembly pipeline from raw sequencing reads to genome assembly
    4. Annotate genomes using various annotation tools and interpret the results

    INTRODUCTION TO BIOINFORMATICS

    Bioinformatics is defined as a data science used to analyze information both stored and processed by living organisms. It involves multiple fields such as biology, data analysis, and computer science. Its main goal is to understand biological processes through the analysis of genomic data. This field revolves greatly around sequence analysis on samples generated by sequencing instruments.

    There are four categories for bioinformatics which are:

    1. Classification: involved in the determination of species composition
    2. Assembly: analysis on a genome's nucleotide composition
    3. Resequencing: identification of variations and mutations
    4. Quantification: determination of a cell's functional characteristics

    The four previously mentioned categories fall under different methods when conducting bioinformatic analysis. An example of classification is through identifying unknown species based on their genetic material. Assembly was the focus of the Human Genome Project which paved the way for the genetic assembly for thousands of other species. Resequencing was conducted to identify changes from already recognized genomes. Focusing on the mutations and variants of DNA that have caused phenotypic changes have helped study important topics such as emerging diseases, changes in fitness, and survival rates. Quantification enabled the understanding of biological processes through gene expression.

    BIOLOGY FOR BIOINFORMATICS

    The study of biology involves multiple fields such as chemistry, ecology, botany, and the like. It aims to understand living organisms and how they function, their origin, taxonomic identity, and chemical structure. Bioinformatics has proven to be a big contributor to the study of Biology as it aids in understanding the complexity involved in the multiple biological sciences.

    DNA

    Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is a macromolecule that contains an individual's genetic information that is needed for its development, function, and reproduction. DNA can either be found in a nucleus or the cytoplasm of a prokaryotic cell. The composition of DNA involves two molecular strands that take the form of alternating molecules of deoxyribose and phosphate. This forms the DNA into a double helix. The strands are joined together by nucleotides, wherein the order dictates the genetic information. These nucleotides have four types: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G) and Cytosine (C). Base pairs are formed by these nucleotides as they are matched in the forms of “A-T,” “T-A,” “G-C,” and “C-G.”

    RNA

    RNA is a similar molecule to DNA that differs in the lack of a base T and instead has a base called Uracil (U). It is single-stranded and has multiple types: mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, and others.

    Proteins

    Through the genome, an organism can conduct processes such as protein production. A protein is composed of a series of amino acids. There are currently 30 identified amino acids which are represented by the letters of the alphabet. Living organisms function based on the protein interaction involved in their cells. Proteins are formed through the translation of the mRNA that partitions into three consecutive letters known as codons translated into amino acids.

    SEQUENCING

    Identification of DNA composition involves DNA Sequencing stored in multiple file formats. The process begins with laboratory processes that transform DNA into a sequencing laboratory. The entire DNA sequence of an organism is found in its genome which is present in each cell. During cell division, the genome replicates into the daughter cells.

    Illumina Next Generation Sequencing

    • Library Preparation — DNA or cDNA samples are used to produce a sequencing library through random fragmentation. Adapters are ligated and tagmentation is done to combine the fragmentation and ligation reactions. These samples then undergo PCR and gel purification.
    • Cluster Generation — The prepared library is loaded into a flow cell containing lawns that capture the DNA fragments. The fragments undergo amplification into clusters via bridge amplification. Once cluster generation is complete, the amplified templates can then be used for sequencing.
    • Sequencing — Single bases are detected as they are incorporated into DNA template strands. This will result in base-by-base sequencing.
    • Data Analysis — The sequences obtained will undergo alignment with a reference genome. Once alignment has been conducted, it will enable downstream analysis. This may include detection of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), indels, read counting, or phylogenetic and metagenomic analysis.

    BASIC CODING

    Bourne-Again Shell (BASH)

    BASH is an acronym for Bourne-Again Shell, a Unix shell that interprets a certain command language. It functions by analyzing and interacting with a collection of codes that will later be executed by the interface. The concept of bash is divided into two definitions: shell and scripting. The shell enables the retrieval, processing, and storage of information. Scripting is collecting these shell commands with the use of a text editor. This text editor will contain the bash commands saved as a .sh extension. Bash scripting is an ideal method for a workflow as it enables the execution of commands through one executable script file.

    Linux

    Software manages hardware resources through an operating system when scripting. The typical operating system for handling bash scripting is Linux. Linux possesses a graphical interface with open-source software that is available to the public. It is highly reliable when conducting bioinformatic practices. One Linux distribution commonly used is Ubuntu.

    Command Line Interface

    A CLI is a text-based interface for initiating commands that relay from the software to the hardware. With CLI, one can run programs, manage files, and interact with the computer. Maneuvering the CLI involves using codes that are typed, prompted, and executed.

    Navigation of Paths

    When using Linux, it is important to understand a path. A path is an environment variable found in the system that aids in detecting a directory or file. There are two types of paths, namely absolute path and relative path. An absolute path is the complete path of a file containing the root element. It is composed of the directory list and a file's specific location. On the other hand, a relative path uses a location about another location, which is typically the current directory.

    Data is a collection of information with the ability to design and optimize it. Data can have multiple properties based on its optimization. It can be represented differently, it can be described in multiple ways, and may also not be readily accessible. Biological data is characterized into the following:

    1. Data that captures prior knowledge (reference data)
    2. Data obtained experimentally
    3. Data produced through analysis

    WHOLE GENOME ASSEMBLY AND ANNOTATION WORKFLOW

    Quality Control

    To ensure the success and reliability of data, one must conduct quality assessment. Sequencing quality scores detect if a base is called incorrectly. This happens as each read is assigned a quality score based on phred score. This quality score appears as a string of characters representing a sequence base. This enables the detection of misidentified bases. To determine the validity of raw reads, a FastQC program may be used. This program is a quality control tool that can either run as a stand-alone interactive application or as a non-interactive program. A FastQC report includes several components summarized below.

    Table 4: Contents of a FastQC File
    FastQC componentDescription
    Per base sequence qualityRepresented as a box-and-whisker plot that shows aggregated quality score statistics of all reads. It is designed as a number of reads vs. average quality score. It is ideal for the read distribution to be in the upper range.
    Per tile sequence qualityIndicates average quality scores per tile across all bases. It checks if there is any loss in quality at parts of the flow cell.
    Per sequence quality scoreA plot of total number of reads vs. average quality score.
    Per base sequence contentReports the called bases of each nucleotide according to their position in the file.
    Per sequence GC contentProvides the GC distribution over all sequences. It is represented by a central peak in the graph.
    Per base N contentShows the percent of bases at each position with no base call, represented by the character “N”.
    Sequence Length DistributionPresents the distribution of read lengths over all sequences.
    Sequence Duplication LevelsExplores the number of duplicated sequences in the library.
    Overrepresented sequencesDisplays the sequences that occur more than 0.1% of the total number of sequences.
    Adapter contentDetects the sequence library adapter at the indicated base position.

    Trimming

    Prior to genome assembly, trimming of the raw reads is recommended, especially if there is presence of sequencing artifacts such as sequencing adapters and/or primers in the raw data. Trimming process can also remove low quality reads to ensure only high quality reads are included for succeeding steps of the pipeline. Overall, trimming improves assembly accuracy and reduces misassemblies, which are important for accurate downstream analyses. Popular trimming programs include trimmomatic, fastp, and cutadapt.

    Genome Assembly

    Genome assembly is the process of reconstructing the entire genome from sequencing reads. This process can be classified into two methods: de novo assembly and reference-based assembly. The choice of method will mainly depend on the target objectives or availability of a reference genome.

    De novo Assembly

    De novo assembly is the method of reconstructing the original sequence without prior knowledge of the correct sequence or the order of the sequences. This method is appropriate for the assembly of novel sequences/genomes. The assembly of short sequences through amplicon sequencing can be done with at least one paired-end library, while multiple libraries are often required to generate long, contiguous quality assemblies for whole-genome assembly. Popular de novo assemblers include SPAdes, Velvet, Megahit, MaSuRCA, ABySS, and SOAPdenovo.

    Popular algorithms for de novo assembly

    A. Overlap Layout Consensus (OLC)

    The OLC approach involves finding overlaps among the reads, finding the layout, ordering all reads using the Hamiltonian path, and creating a consensus sequence. Since this method mainly relies on the overlaps of the reads, it is suitable for long-read sequence data. The steps for the OLC method are the following:

    1. Overlap — find the overlaps among reads. Each read is considered as a node while the edges (indicated by the arrows) represent the overlaps between reads.
    2. Layout — determine the best ordering of the reads using the Hamiltonian path. The Hamiltonian path is the path in which all nodes (or reads) can be visited only once.
    3. Consensus — determine the majority nucleotide bases in multiple sequence alignment of ordered reads.

    B. De Bruijn graph (DBG)

    In the de Bruijn graph approach, substrings of reads with length k (called a k-mer) are used for the assembly. The graph is then constructed and simplified using the collections of k-mers as nodes and overlaps as edges. Example:

    1. Create 3-mers of sequence actagtagc → act, cta, tag, agt, gta, tag, agc
    2. Using these 3-mers, create edges indicating overlaps between reads with k−1 bases
    3. Simplify by identifying and merging identical k-mers
    4. Identify the Eulerian path, or the best path to travel all edges once
    5. Generate contigs

    In the de Bruijn graph, choosing the right k-mer length for the sample is important. With a very short k-mer length, the graph becomes very complicated, while a very long k-mer length would result in a fragmented graph.

    Reference-Based Assembly

    Reference-based assembly provides a means to obtain high-quality genome sequences. It is used when a species is well-characterized, and a reliable genome exists. Sample sequences are aligned to the reference, allowing comparison between genome structures by their similarities and differences. The main goal of reference-based assembly is to align the genome sequences of a sample to a known template. This makes it a computationally efficient way to assemble the genome of a target organism. Once aligned with a reference, comparisons can be made, and SNPs or indels can be detected. This type of assembly can be applied to comparative genomics, population studies, medicine, or agriculture.

    Reference-based assembly mapping tools

    A. Burrows-Wheeler Aligner (BWA)

    The major functionality of this algorithm is to construct a suffix array based on the reads and apply BWT to compress and index the genome. It is compact and memory-efficient due to BWT compression. This method is optimized for short reads to align to the reference genome, making it more accurate in detecting exact matches. It does perform well with smaller genome sizes like bacteria and archaea.

    B. Minimap2

    This method begins by selecting an appropriate k-mer size, similar to de novo assemblers, but uses the k-mers for efficient read alignment to a reference genome. It then employs minimizer-based indexing for data compression, enabling accurate alignment of long reads, particularly for high-error-rate data like Nanopore sequencing.

    Disadvantages of reference-based assembly

    Despite the high accuracy and good quality results reference-based assembly can offer, there are also some disadvantages. If samples are highly divergent or poorly characterized, it may not be picked up through the reference genome. The data may appear inaccurate if a reference is unavailable or extremely different. It will also be difficult to capture any novel organisms.

    Reference Genome

    A reference genome is a complete, representative DNA sequence of a species, assembled and curated to serve as a standard for comparison. For example, the human reference genome provides a baseline against which other DNA sequences can be aligned and analyzed, enabling the identification of genetic variants, mutations, and structural differences.

    Genome Assembly Quality Assessment

    Evaluating the quality of assembled genomes is a crucial step in whole genome sequencing, ensuring reliability by assessing metrics such as contiguity, completeness, coverage, and annotation potential. This ensures that the assembled genome is reliable and suitable for downstream analysis.

    QUAST

    QUAST (QUality ASsessment Tool) computes basic assembly statistics, including the number of contigs, assembly length, contiguity metrics (N50 values), and coverage (aligned and unaligned sequences) to detect potential assembly errors.

    Table 5: Key QUAST Metrics
    MetricDescriptionIdeal Value
    # ContigsTotal number of contigs in the assemblyLower is better
    Total LengthSum of all contig lengths (bp)Close to expected genome size
    Largest ContigLength of the longest contig (bp)As large as possible
    N50Contig length at 50% of total assemblyHigher is better
    N75Contig length at 75% of total assemblyHigher is better
    L50Min. contigs covering 50% of assemblyLower is better
    L75Min. contigs covering 75% of assemblyLower is better
    GC Content (%)Guanine-Cytosine percentageClose to expected for species
    # N's per 100 kbpNumber of ambiguous bases per 100 kbpClose to 0
    Genome Fraction (%)Percentage of reference genome covered (reference-based)Close to 100%
    # MisassembliesStructural errors vs. reference (reference-based)Close to 0
    # Mismatches per 100 kbpSingle-base errors per 100 kbp (reference-based)Close to 0
    # Indels per 100 kbpInsertions/deletions per 100 kbp (reference-based)Close to 0

    Quality interpretation:

    • Contiguity: Fewer contigs and a higher N50 indicate a more contiguous assembly.
    • Completeness: Total assembly length should be close to the expected genome size; significantly larger may indicate redundancy or contamination.
    • Accuracy: Low misassemblies, mismatches, and indels (reference mode) indicate high structural and base-level accuracy.
    • Gaps: N's per 100 kbp close to 0 indicates fewer unresolved regions.
    • GC Content: Deviation from expected GC% may indicate contamination from other organisms.
    Note

    QUAST metrics should always be interpreted alongside other quality assessments (e.g., BUSCO). A highly contiguous assembly (high N50) is not necessarily complete or accurate — it may still be missing genes or contain misassemblies.

    BUSCO

    BUSCO (Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs) is one of the most widely used metrics for checking the overall completeness of genome assemblies, gene sets, and transcriptomes by comparing them against evolutionarily conserved single-copy orthologs that are nearly universal across specific lineages.

    BUSCO outputs include summary statistics of complete single-copy orthologs (C), single-copy (S), duplicated (D), fragmented (F), missing (M), and the total BUSCO. A high proportion of complete BUSCOs indicates a high-quality genome assembly.

    Table 6: BUSCO Categories

    • Excellent: C > 95%, M < 2%

    Genome Annotation

    Genome annotation is a critical process that identifies and labels the functional components within a genomic sequence. This step is essential because DNA sequencing produces sequences whose functions are initially unknown. The genome annotation process includes several key steps: identification of genomic features, functional annotation, assignment of gene ontology terms, pathway analysis, and comparative genomic analysis. Genome annotation can be broadly divided into two categories:

    • Structural annotation — identifying the physical locations and boundaries of genomic features such as genes, open reading frames (ORFs), coding sequences (CDS), tRNAs, rRNAs, and regulatory elements.
    • Functional annotation — assigning biological functions, gene names, product descriptions, and pathway associations to the identified features.

    PGAP

    PGAP (Prokaryotic Genome Annotation Pipeline) is the annotation pipeline developed and maintained by the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI). It is the standard pipeline used to annotate prokaryotic genomes submitted to GenBank and RefSeq databases. PGAP produces consistent, high-quality annotations that follow NCBI's standardized naming conventions, making it the preferred choice for genomes intended for public database submission.

    Key Features

    • Uses GeneMarkS-2+ for ab initio gene prediction.
    • Performs homology-based annotation against NCBI's curated protein databases.
    • Identifies CDS, rRNAs, tRNAs, ncRNAs, CRISPR arrays, and pseudogenes.
    • Assigns protein names following NCBI naming standards.
    • Produces GenBank-compatible output files ready for submission.
    • Runs via Docker/Singularity container for reproducibility.
    Table 8: PGAP Output Files

    annot.gbkGenBankFull annotation with sequences and features
    annot.gffGFF3Genomic feature coordinates and attributes
    annot.faaFASTAPredicted protein sequences
    annot.fnaFASTAAnnotated nucleotide sequences
    annot.sqnSequinFile for GenBank submission
    calls.tabTabularSummary of all feature calls
    When to use PGAP

    Recommended: When submitting genomes to NCBI GenBank/RefSeq; when standardized, reproducible annotations are required; for reference-grade genome projects.
    Considerations: Requires Docker or Singularity; computationally heavier than PROKKA; less customizable for non-standard organisms.

    PROKKA

    PROKKA is a fast, lightweight command-line tool for annotating bacterial, archaeal, and viral genomes. Developed by Torsten Seemann, PROKKA is widely used in the research community for its speed and ease of use. It coordinates multiple external tools to predict and annotate genomic features in a single pipeline.

    Key Features

    • Rapid annotation — typically completes in under 10 minutes per genome.
    • Integrates multiple prediction tools in a hierarchical manner.
    • Produces a comprehensive set of output files in standard formats.
    • Customizable with user-supplied protein databases for improved annotation.
    • Lightweight with minimal computational requirements.

    PROKKA Annotation Hierarchy

    PROKKA assigns protein function using a hierarchical database search strategy, proceeding from the most reliable to least reliable source. If a match is found at a higher level, the search stops:

    1. User-supplied proteins — Custom trusted protein database (if provided via --proteins).
    2. UniProt/Swiss-Prot — Curated, high-quality protein database.
    3. RefSeq — NCBI Reference Sequence protein clusters.
    4. Pfam — Protein family HMM profiles.
    5. TIGRFAM — TIGR protein family HMM profiles.
    6. Hypothetical protein — Default label if no match is found.
    When to use PROKKA

    Recommended: For rapid, routine annotation of prokaryotic genomes; comparative genomics studies involving many genomes; as input for pan-genome analysis tools (e.g., Roary, Panaroo).
    Considerations: Annotations may not fully comply with NCBI naming standards; not officially supported for GenBank submissions (use PGAP instead).

    conda config --add channels bioconda

    This updates your conda configuration so packages from these channels can be found automatically.

    Understanding Conda Environments

    A conda environment is an isolated workspace for a specific tool. Here's the basic workflow:

    conda create -n fastqc       # Step 1: Create the environment
conda activate fastqc        # Step 2: Activate the environment
conda install fastqc         # Step 3: Install the tool

    The commands below combine steps 1 and 2, creating the environment and installing the tool in one command using the -c (channel) and -y (auto-confirm) flags.

    How to Find and Install Packages

    Before installing any tool, you can search for it on anaconda.org to find the correct installation command:

    1. Visit anaconda.org
    2. Search for your tool (e.g., "fastqc")
    3. Look for packages from trusted channels like bioconda or conda-forge
    4. Copy the installation command provided

    Installation syntax example:

    conda install bioconda::fastqc

    The channel::package syntax directly specifies which channel to use. For bioinformatics tools, bioconda is the primary channel, while conda-forge provides supporting dependencies.

    Introduction: Quality control is the first step in any sequencing analysis. FastQC evaluates the quality of your raw reads, identifying potential issues like adapters, low-quality bases, or contamination. MultiQC aggregates these results into a single report.

    Run FastQC on All Read Files

    Activate FastQC and run quality check on all fastq files:

    Step 2: Trim Reads

    Introduction: Read trimming removes low-quality bases, adapters, and short fragments. This improves assembly quality by removing problematic data before assembly.

    Illumina ReadsR1 + R2

    Trim Illumina Short Reads Using fastp

    Activate the fastp environment and trim your short read files. fastp removes adapters, trims low-quality bases, and filters short reads:

    conda activate PGCV_fastp
    fastp -i illumina_R1.fastq.gz -I illumina_R2.fastq.gz \
        -o illumina_R1.trim.fastq.gz -O illumina_R2.trim.fastq.gz \
        -h fastp.html -j fastp.json -w 4 -q 30 -l 35

    -i and -I are input files (forward and reverse). -o and -O are output files (trimmed reads). -w 4 uses 4 threads.

    Filter Nanopore Long Reads Using NanoFilt

    Switch to the NanoFilt environment and filter long reads by quality and minimum length:

    conda activate PGCV_nanofilt
    zcat nanopore.fastq.gz | NanoFilt -q 7 --length 1000 | \
        gzip > nanopore.filtered.fastq.gz

    -q 7 keeps only reads with quality score 7 or higher. --length 1000 filters out reads shorter than 1000 bases.

    Output Files Created:

    • illumina_R2.trim.fastq.gz — Trimmed reverse reads
    • nanopore.filtered.fastq.gz — Filtered nanopore reads
    • fastp.html — Trimming statistics and visualization
    • fastp.json — Trimming statistics in JSON format
    Guide Questions:

    Step 4: Assembly

    Introduction: Genome assembly reconstructs the full genome from short read fragments. We provide three strategies depending on your data type and goals: short-read-only, hybrid (combining short and long reads), or long reads assembly and polished using short reads.

    Trimmed Reads
    Choose AssemblyStrategy
    Short reads only
    SPAdes
    assembly.fasta
    Hybrid
    UnicyclerShort + Long
    assembly.fasta
    Long-read + polish
    FlyeLong Reads
    Initial Assembly
    PolishPilon + Short Reads
    assembly.fasta

    Option A: Short Reads Only (SPAdes)

    Use only Illumina short reads for assembly:

    conda activate PGCV_spades
    spades.py -1 illumina_R1.trim.fastq.gz -2 illumina_R2.trim.fastq.gz \
          -o spades_out -t 8 -m 32

    -1 and -2 are forward and reverse reads. -t 8 uses 8 threads. -m 32 uses 32 GB of memory.

    Option B: Hybrid Assembly (Unicycler)

    Combine both Illumina short reads and nanopore long reads for better assembly:

    conda activate PGCV_unicycler
    unicycler -1 illumina_R1.trim.fastq.gz -2 illumina_R2.trim.fastq.gz \
          -l nanopore.filtered.fastq.gz -o unicycler_out -t 8

    -1 and -2 are paired-end Illumina reads. -l is long reads from nanopore. This hybrid approach often produces better results.

    Option C: Long reads assembly and polished using short reads (Flye + Pilon)

    Assemble with Flye using built-in polishing (-i 3 for 3 polishing iterations):

    conda activate PGCV_flye
    flye --nano-raw nanopore.filtered.fastq.gz -o flye_out -t 8 -i 3

    First, create an index for the assembly. This allows bwa to quickly search the assembly:

    conda activate PGCV_bwa
    bwa index flye_out/assembly.fasta

    Next, align the Illumina reads to the Flye assembly. bwa compares each read to the assembly:

    bwa mem -t 8 flye_out/assembly.fasta \
          illumina_R1.trim.fastq.gz illumina_R2.trim.fastq.gz \
          > short_on_flye.sam

    -t 8 uses 8 threads. The output is a SAM file showing where each read aligns.

    Convert the SAM file to BAM format (compressed) and sort it. Then index it for quick lookup:

    conda activate PGCV_samtools
    samtools view -bS short_on_flye.sam | samtools sort -@ 8 -o short_on_flye.bam
samtools index short_on_flye.bam

    view -bS converts SAM to BAM. sort organizes alignments by position. index creates a quick lookup file.

    Finally, use Pilon to correct errors in the Flye assembly using the aligned short reads:

    conda activate PGCV_pilon
pilon --genome flye_out/assembly.fasta --bam short_on_flye.bam \
  --outdir pilon_out --threads 8

    Pilon reads the aligned short reads and fixes base errors in the assembly.

    Final polished assembly: pilon_out/pilon.fasta

    Step 6: Evaluate Completeness with BUSCO

    Introduction: BUSCO assesses genome completeness by checking for the presence of conserved single-copy genes. A high BUSCO score indicates a more complete assembly.

    Run BUSCO for Bacterial Genomes

    Activate BUSCO and check for conserved bacterial genes in your assembly:

    conda activate PGCV_busco
    busco -i assemblies/unicycler_assembly.fasta -l bacteria_odb10 \
        -m genome -o busco_out -c 8 -f

    -l bacteria_odb10 specifies the gene database for bacteria. BUSCO searches for these genes to estimate completeness.

    Output Summary

    Output Directory: busco_out/
    • short_summary.*.busco.txt — Summary of BUSCO results with percentages
    • full_table.tsv — Detailed gene hits
    • missing_busco_list.tsv — Missing genes
    Guide Questions:
    • What percentage of BUSCO genes were found (complete + fragmented)?
    • How many were completely matched?
    • How many were missing?
    • Does this indicate a complete assembly of your target genome?
